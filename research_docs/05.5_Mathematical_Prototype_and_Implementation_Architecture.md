# 05.5 - Mathematical Prototype and Implementation Architecture

> **Project:** Bayesian Online Listwise Ranking (BOLR)  
> **Purpose:** Define the implementation architecture for the first mathematically serious BOLR system while preserving a direct path toward the intended advanced production model.  
> **Status:** Implementation specification and future-design ledger  
> **Depends on:** `01`, `01.5`, `02`, `04`, and `05`

---

# 1. Purpose

The next stage is not another disposable proof of concept. It is the beginning of one extensible BOLR reference implementation that will serve as:

1. the mathematical correctness oracle;
2. the synthetic-testing environment;
3. the historical prequential research framework;
4. the reference against which future C kernels are checked;
5. the foundation from which the final sophisticated system evolves.

The development principle is:

$$
\boxed{
\text{design broadly}
\rightarrow
\text{activate conservatively}
\rightarrow
\text{validate rigorously}
\rightarrow
\text{extend selectively}
}
$$

Early configurations are simple **by activation**, not simple **by architecture**.

---

# 2. Long-Term Model Vision

The long-term score may eventually be:

$$
\begin{aligned}
s_{it}
={}&f_{\text{surface}}(c_i,m_t)
+f_{\text{history}}(z_{it},m_t)\\
&+r_{\text{graph},it}
+f_{\text{regime}}(c_i,k_t)
+b_{i,t}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Potential advanced components include:

- contextual tensor surfaces;
- rolling candidate-performance corrections;
- graph-Laplacian or graph-TV residuals;
- blockwise process noise;
- partitioned-preference observations;
- direct robust utility modelling;
- BOCPD-controlled adaptation;
- a low-dimensional Rao--Blackwellised particle state;
- Kronecker, low-rank, block, or sparse posterior covariance;
- posterior region probabilities for downstream allocation;
- a C-optimised numerical backend.

These are recorded as the design horizon, not as requirements for the first milestone.

# 3. First Reference Configuration

The first historical model should use:

$$
s_{it}
=
\phi_c(c_i)^\top\alpha
+
\phi_c(c_i)^\top B_t\psi(m_t).
$$

Where:

- $\alpha$ is a static baseline suitability surface;
- $B_t$ is the dynamic context-to-surface deformation;
- $\phi_c(c_i)$ is a centred tensor-product configuration basis;
- $\psi(m_t)$ is a compact market-context basis.

The dynamic state is:

$$
\theta_t=\operatorname{vec}(B_t).
$$

The initial system uses:

- dense Gaussian posterior covariance;
- Gaussian random-walk or discount evolution;
- Laplace filtering;
- tolerance-aware soft-target generalized Bayes;
- strict prequential evaluation.

Initially excluded:

- dynamic graph residual;
- BOCPD;
- particle filtering;
- dynamic rolling-performance coefficients;
- adaptive daily learning rate;
- direct-utility covariance modelling;
- gated experts;
- RFF.

They are deferred, not rejected.

---

# 4. Proposed Initial Dimensions

Use a configurable tensor-product basis.

A sensible first setting is:

$$
A=6,\qquad B=8,
$$

so:

$$
p_c=AB=48.
$$

Use a compact context basis:

$$
p_m=6.
$$

Then:

$$
P=p_cp_m=288
$$

dynamic parameters.

This is large enough to represent a meaningful moving suitability surface while remaining feasible for a dense-covariance reference filter.

Later sweeps should include:

$$
p_c\in\{24,48,80,120\},
$$

and:

$$
p_m\in\{4,6,10,16\}.
$$

No code should assume the defaults are permanent.

A recommended logical structure is:

```text
bolr/
  config/
  data/
  representation/
  targets/
  observations/
  dynamics/
  posterior/
  inference/
  synthetic/
  evaluation/
  checkpoint/
  numerics/
  backends/
  tests/
  experiments/
```

Core responsibilities:

## `representation`

Produces:

$$
s_t=b_t+X_t\theta_t.
$$

## `targets`

Converts utility vectors into:

- soft distributions;
- ordered groups;
- direct utility observations;
- no-update events.

## `observations`

Provides log factors, gradients, and curvature.

## `dynamics`

Maps the previous posterior into the predictive prior.

## `inference`

Combines predictive priors and observation factors.

## `synthetic`

Provides known-truth scenarios for validation.

## `evaluation`

Runs strict prequential experiments and computes business, ranking, and calibration metrics.

## `backends`

Allows NumPy and C kernels to share the same mathematical interface.

# 7. Candidate Coordinates and Basis

For configuration $i$:

$$
u_i=\log(\text{entry percentage}_i),
$$

$$
v_i=\log(\text{sl-trail percentage}_i).
$$

Standardise them using fixed training-derived parameters.

Construct:

$$
\phi_e(u_i)\in\mathbb R^A,
$$

$$
\phi_r(v_i)\in\mathbb R^B,
$$

and:

$$
\phi_c(c_i)
=
\phi_e(u_i)\otimes\phi_r(v_i).
$$

The fixed candidate-basis matrix is:

$$
\Phi_c\in\mathbb R^{N\times p_c}.
$$

The first basis should be tensor-product B-splines with configurable:

- degree;
- knot placement;
- boundary handling;
- basis dimension;
- centring.

## Future basis plans

- multi-resolution splines;
- radial basis functions;
- Fourier or wavelet bases;
- graph eigenvectors;
- modular RFF;
- basis enrichment using trade-mechanics geometry.

---

# 8. Basis Centring and Identifiability

Centre the candidate basis so that:

$$
\mathbf 1^\top\Phi_c=0.
$$

This removes candidate-independent score shifts from the dynamic contextual term.

The system should preserve both:

- the original basis;
- the centring transform.

Future alternatives include:

- explicit sum-to-zero score projection;
- constrained optimisation;
- orthonormal basis construction;
- reference-candidate coding.

The centred-basis approach is preferred initially.

---

# 11. Dynamic Design

For day $t$:

$$
X_t
=
\psi(m_t)^\top\otimes\Phi_c.
$$

Equivalently:

$$
X_t\theta_t
=
\Phi_cB_t\psi(m_t).
$$

The first backend may explicitly construct $X_t$ for transparency.

A later backend should exploit structure:

1. reshape $\theta_t$ into $B_t$;
2. compute $a_t=B_t\psi(m_t)$;
3. compute $\Phi_ca_t$.

## Future design plans

- low-rank $B_t$;
- matrix-normal posterior;
- Kronecker-factor covariance;
- operator-only Hessian products;
- dynamic low-dimensional factor amplitudes.

# 13. Candidate A: Soft-Target Generalized Bayes

Transform utility:

$$
v_{it}=g_t(u_{it}).
$$

Initial transform candidates:

1. median/MAD scaling with clipping;
2. rank transformation;
3. ordinal-grade scores.

Then:

$$
r_{it}
=
\frac{\exp(\kappa v_{it})}
{\sum_j\exp(\kappa v_{jt})}.
$$

The model probabilities are:

$$
q_{it}
=
\frac{\exp(s_{it})}
{\sum_j\exp(s_{jt})}.
$$

The loss is:

$$
\mathcal L_t
=
-\sum_i r_{it}\log q_{it}.
$$

The generalized observation factor is:

$$
L_t=\exp(-\eta\mathcal L_t).
$$

## Future Candidate A plans

- density-corrected target mass;
- entropy-constrained target construction;
- top-region mass allocation;
- adaptive $\kappa$;
- calibrated adaptive $\eta_t$;
- geometry-aware target smoothing.

---

# 14. Candidate A Derivatives

Score gradient:

$$
\nabla_s\mathcal L=q-r.
$$

Score Hessian:

$$
\nabla_s^2\mathcal L
=
\operatorname{Diag}(q)-qq^\top.
$$

Parameter gradient:

$$
\nabla_\theta\mathcal L
=
X^\top(q-r).
$$

Parameter Hessian:

$$
\nabla_\theta^2\mathcal L
=
X^\top
\left[
\operatorname{Diag}(q)-qq^\top
\right]
X.
$$

Do not form the dense $N\times N$ score Hessian.

Use:

$$
X^\top\operatorname{Diag}(q)X
-
(X^\top q)(X^\top q)^\top.
$$

Hessian-vector products should also be implemented.

# 15. Candidate B: Ordered Groups

The initial structure is:

$$
G_H\succ G_M\succ G_L.
$$

Possible high group:

$$
G_H
=
\{i:u_i\ge u_{\max}-\varepsilon_t\},
$$

or:

$$
G_H
=
\left\{
i:u_i\ge\max(\tau_{\text{abs}},u_{\max}-\varepsilon_t)
\right\}.
$$

Possible middle group:

$$
G_M=\{i:u_i>0\}\setminus G_H.
$$

Lower group:

$$
G_L=\{i:u_i\le0\}.
$$

No within-group order is imposed.

$$

=
\sum_{a<b}w_{ab}
\sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal P_{ab}}

# 19. Dynamics

Initial random walk:

$$
\theta_t
=
\theta_{t-1}+\epsilon_t,
$$

$$
\epsilon_t\sim N(0,qI).
$$

Therefore:

$$
m_t^-=m_{t-1}^+,
$$

$$
P_t^-=P_{t-1}^++qI.
$$

Alternative initial dynamics:

$$
P_t^-=\frac{1}{\delta}P_{t-1}^+.
$$

The first comparison is scalar $q$ versus scalar $\delta$.

## Future dynamics plans

- blockwise process noise;
- mean reversion;
- heavy-tailed innovations;
- state-dependent drift;
- BOCPD-controlled inflation;
- partial resets;
- regime-dependent transitions.

# 21. Laplace Filter

For predictive prior:

$$
\theta\sim N(m^-,P^-),
$$

and observation factor $L_t(\theta)$:

$$
\log\widetilde p(\theta)
=
-\frac12(\theta-m^-)^\top(P^-)^{-1}(\theta-m^-)
+
\log L_t(\theta).
$$

Find:

$$
\hat\theta
=
\arg\max_\theta\log\widetilde p(\theta).
$$

Set:

$$
m^+=\hat\theta,
$$

$$
P^+
=
\left[
(P^-)^{-1}
-
\nabla_\theta^2\log L_t(\hat\theta)
\right]^{-1}.
$$

# 22. Newton Solver Requirements

The solver should support:

- predictive-mean warm start;
- analytic gradient;
- analytic Hessian or HVP;
- damping;
- backtracking line search;
- Cholesky solves;
- jitter escalation;
- objective monotonicity checks;
- maximum iterations;
- gradient and step convergence tests;
- structured diagnostics;
- safe fallback.

Fallback sequence:

1. increase damping;
2. increase jitter;
3. retry with smaller step;
4. preserve the predictive prior and flag the update.

A failed update must never corrupt the last valid checkpoint.

# 27. Historical Prequential Engine

For day $t$:

1. load posterior from $t-1$;
2. propagate dynamics;
3. construct $X_t$ and $b_t$;
4. predict before reading day-$t$ utility;
5. save prediction and selected candidate;
6. reveal the full utility vector;
7. construct the observation;
8. update once;
9. record diagnostics;
10. checkpoint.

The runner must enforce leakage safety structurally.

# 34. Future Component Ledger

## Graph residual

**Purpose:** preserve threshold boundaries and local plateaus.  
**Candidates:** Laplacian Gaussian, graph TV, heavy-tailed edge priors.  
**Risk:** large dynamic state and residual re-learning the whole surface.

## BOCPD

**Purpose:** detect context drift or predictive mismatch and alter adaptation.  
**Candidates:** market-feature monitor, ranking-loss monitor, utility-residual monitor.  
**Risk:** false resets and double counting uncertainty.

## RBPF

**Purpose:** sample a small regime, change state, or process-noise multiplier while Gaussian-filtering coefficients.  
**Risk:** multiplying filter cost and regime non-identifiability.

## Direct robust utility

**Purpose:** preserve cardinal utility and estimate expected utility.  
**Candidates:** Student-$t$ with diagonal, low-rank, graph, or hybrid covariance.  
**Risk:** top-list misalignment and covariance misspecification.

## Structured posterior covariance

**Purpose:** scale beyond dense covariance while preserving important dependence.  
**Candidates:** block, low rank plus diagonal, Kronecker, sparse precision.  
**Risk:** approximation bias and under-dispersion.

## Low-rank dynamic surface

**Purpose:** reduce state dimension and accelerate adaptation.  
**Candidate:** $B_t=U\operatorname{diag}(h_t)V^\top$.  
**Risk:** under-rank bias and non-identifiability.

## Multi-asset hierarchy

**Purpose:** share information across instruments.  
**Candidate:** $B_{a,t}=B_{\text{global},t}+B_{\text{asset},t}$.  
**Risk:** negative transfer and much larger inference.

## Continuous configuration optimisation

**Purpose:** score unseen configurations and refine the grid.  
**Risk:** extrapolation and mismatch with tradable configurations.

# 40. Final Principle

The BOLR reference implementation should become progressively more sophisticated without losing mathematical auditability.

The project avoids both:

- disposable toy implementations;
- an untestable all-at-once system.

The intended path is:

$$
\boxed{
\text{one final-shaped architecture}
+
\text{incrementally activated advanced components}
+
\text{permanent correctness references}
}
$$

This preserves the goal of constructing a sophisticated, meticulously engineered model while ensuring that every advanced subsystem is individually understood and justified.
