# Structured Contextual Representations for a Dynamic Bayesian Ranking Surface on a Two-Dimensional Configuration Grid

## Problem framing for BOLR

Your notes already imply a very specific modelling regime, and that regime should drive method choice more than generic ML fashion. BOLR is not a contextual bandit: it is a **full-information, once-daily, contextual online ranking problem** with same-day delayed feedback, a fixed candidate set, and a geometrically spaced two-dimensional configuration grid over entry distance and trailing stop. The configuration space is structurally small in geometry but large in candidates: the working notes describe a complete \(34\times 42\) grid, i.e. \(N=1428\) configurations, with roughly 4.5k daily contexts, while an earlier stage document still uses approximate placeholders around 1,500 candidates and 4,489 days. The important point is unchanged across the notes: the effective sample size is the number of days, not the row count, and every successful model must exploit grid geometry while allowing market context to change *relative* suitability rather than adding a shared scalar that cancels inside a listwise softmax. fileciteturn0file0 fileciteturn0file1 fileciteturn0file2 fileciteturn0file3

The EDA findings sharpen the design target. Neighbouring configurations are often very similar, with a mean normalised neighbour PnL difference around \(0.023\), an identical-neighbour fraction around \(35\%\), a largest-plateau fraction near \(17\%\), and broad connected top regions inside a day; but the *exact* best configuration moves very quickly across days, with day-to-day best changes on about \(98.6\%\) of transitions and low top-set overlap. That means BOLR needs a model class that can represent **broad high-quality regions, fast contextual deformation, and occasional sharp boundaries**. In other words, the statistical target is not “find one static optimum” but “track a context-dependent suitability surface whose favourable region drifts and sometimes fragments.” That immediately disfavors methods that are either globally too smooth or too local and unstructured. fileciteturn0file1 fileciteturn0file2

Throughout the report I will use the notation \(c_i=(u_i,v_i)\) for log-entry and log-stop coordinates, \(\psi(m_t)\in\mathbb R^{H}\) for a market-context basis, and \(z_{it}\) for candidate-specific dynamic summaries such as rolling PnL and win-rate features already documented in your notes. The central engineering question is then: how should one model
\[
s_{it}=s_t(c_i)=f(c_i,m_t)+g(z_{it},m_t),
\]
so that the grid structure is used, the surface can move with market regime, uncertainty is available for downstream gating, and overnight updating remains computationally realistic on commodity hardware? fileciteturn0file0 fileciteturn0file2

## Separable smoothers and low-rank surfaces

**Tensor-product splines and basis expansions.** A natural baseline is
\[
s_{it}=\phi_e(u_i)^\top W_t \phi_r(v_i)+\gamma^\top z_{it},
\]
or, with context-varying coefficients,
\[
W_t=W_0+\sum_{h=1}^{H}\psi_h(m_t)\,W_h.
\]
This is the tensor-product smooth/GAM view: a low- to moderate-dimensional basis on each axis, then a Kronecker-style tensor basis on the grid. Its main assumptions are that the latent surface is *mostly smooth in coordinate space* and that anisotropy can be handled by different bases or penalties on the two axes. Market context modifies the surface cleanly by shifting the coefficient tensor rather than by adding a day-constant intercept, so ranking can actually change across candidates. Plateaus are representable if the basis is moderately rich, but true mechanical discontinuities are usually blurred unless the basis is made very flexible or combined with a discontinuity-aware term. Interpolation within the observed grid is excellent; extrapolation outside the grid exists but should not be trusted operationally. Bayesian uncertainty is straightforward because spline penalties are equivalent to Gaussian priors on coefficients, and reduced-rank spline/GAM formulations support approximate Gaussian posteriors and empirical-Bayes or full-Bayes smoothing-parameter treatment. Sequential updating is feasible if the observation model is Gaussianized or handled with Laplace/ADF-style filtering over coefficients. Parameter count is \(AB(1+H)\) for a varying-coefficient surface with \(A\) entry bases and \(B\) stop bases; for example \(A=8\), \(B=10\), \(H=12\) gives \(80\times 13=1040\) surface coefficients before shrinkage. Per-day scoring is \(O(NAB)\) if fully explicit but can be reduced using precomputed basis matrices; memory is modest. Main identifiability issues are the listwise translation invariance of daily scores and confounding between surface scale and likelihood temperature. Likely failure modes are over-smoothing at decision boundaries, basis under-resolution, or context interaction overfitting when \(H\) is too large relative to the roughly 4.5k daily contexts. For BOLR this is a **strong first-line method**, because it matches the near-regular log-grid and gives the cleanest interpolation behaviour. The right prototype is a contextual tensor spline with REML-style shrinkage or Bayesian Gaussian priors, compared at \(A\times B\in\{6\times 8,8\times 10,10\times 12\}\), with and without a dynamic residual term. fileciteturn0file2 citeturn15search0turn16search1turn16academia3

**Low-rank tensor surfaces.** Here the coefficient surface is factorised:
\[
s_{it}=\sum_{r=1}^{R} a_r(u_i)\,b_r(v_i)\,g_r(m_t)+\gamma^\top z_{it},
\]
or, in matrix form, \(W_t\approx U\,\mathrm{diag}(G_t)\,V^\top\). The central assumption is that the grid surface is generated by a small number of dominant interaction modes rather than a full-rank tensor. This is attractive when the “good region” mainly moves, rotates, broadens, or narrows in a low-dimensional way across days. Market context enters by modulating the low-rank factors \(g_r(m_t)\) or by making one factor matrix context-dependent. Plateaus are representable if they align with a small number of smooth modes; localized discontinuities are usually hard unless rank increases or a residual is added. Interpolation is good so long as the one-dimensional factors \(a_r(u)\) and \(b_r(v)\) are smooth. Bayesian uncertainty can be placed on factor loadings and context modulators, but posterior geometry is harder than for ordinary splines because factorisations are non-identifiable: sign flips, rotations, and scale exchanges leave the same product unchanged. Sequential updating is possible but more fragile; you usually want strong priors, orthogonality/ordering constraints, or to filter a full coefficient matrix with a low-rank prior rather than filter unconstrained factors directly. Parameter count is roughly \(R(A+B+H)\) for a simple context-modulated factorisation; with \(A=8\), \(B=10\), \(H=12\), \(R=4\), this is about \(120\) structured parameters, far smaller than the spline tensor above. Runtime is correspondingly light: per-day scoring can be \(O(NR)\) after basis projection. The failure mode is under-rank bias: broad plateaus are captured, but a narrow profitable ridge or stop-trigger discontinuity is missed. For BOLR, low-rank surfaces are best viewed as a **compression device for the smooth component**, not as the whole model. The right prototype is to compare full contextual splines against rank-\(R\in\{2,4,6,8\}\) factored surfaces under identical observations and drift processes, measuring whether the rank constraint improves generalisation enough to offset misspecification. fileciteturn0file2 citeturn5academia5turn6academia1turn6academia0

**Kronecker-structured kernels and covariances.** A separable Gaussian prior or GP on the grid has
\[
K_c\big((u,v),(u',v')\big)=K_e(u,u')\,K_r(v,v'),
\]
so that \(K_c=K_e\otimes K_r\) on the Cartesian grid. This assumes that similarity decomposes multiplicatively across entry and stop coordinates and that the grid is sufficiently regular to exploit Kronecker algebra. Market context can modify the surface through a contextual mean function,
\[
\mu_t(c)=\phi_c(c)^\top B\psi(m_t),
\]
through context-dependent kernel hyperparameters, or through a product kernel \(K_c\cdot K_m\) over \((c,m)\). The strength of the method is Bayesian uncertainty: posterior mean, variance, and correlation structure are all principled. Interpolation is also excellent. Plateaus are easy if the kernel length-scales are long enough; abrupt discontinuities are not, unless you add a local residual, a change-surface component, or a sum of kernels with shorter scales. Sequential updating is feasible with sparse/inducing or state-space approximations, but exact non-Gaussian listwise updates are not cheap. The computational attraction is that operations on an \(n_e\times n_r\) grid can be reduced from dense \(O(N^3)\) algebra to Kronecker-enabled eigendecompositions and matrix-vector products involving the smaller factors; scalable GP work shows why this can be dramatically cheaper than unstructured covariance handling on grid-like domains. Hyperparameter count is tiny, but latent-state count depends on whether you represent the full grid, an inducing grid, or a basis expansion. Identifiability problems arise when mean and covariance both try to explain the same smooth structure, and when separability is too restrictive. The main failure mode for BOLR is that a *purely separable* covariance is elegant but often too rigid for surfaces where stop width matters differently at small versus large entry distances. For BOLR, Kronecker structure is best used as an **inference-enabling prior for smooth Gaussian blocks**, especially if combined with a nonseparable residual. The prototype experiment should compare a contextual mean + separable GP residual against the same mean + independent residual, using a reduced inducing grid such as \(16\times 16\) or \(20\times 20\), and recording both calibration and overnight runtime. fileciteturn0file2 citeturn7academia0turn0academia3turn7academia1turn17academia1

## Graph-based and discontinuity-aware priors

**Graph-Laplacian smoothing.** Represent the configuration grid as a graph \(G=(V,E)\) with one node per configuration and edges between direct neighbours. A standard prior is
\[
p(s_t)\propto \exp\!\left(-\frac{\lambda}{2}(s_t-\mu_t)^\top L (s_t-\mu_t)\right),
\]
where \(L\) is the graph Laplacian and \(\mu_t\) may be a contextual mean surface. The assumption is local smoothness on the observed grid, not necessarily a globally smooth coordinate function. That is a very good match to your EDA: it formalises “nearby configurations should usually be similar” without requiring a continuous off-grid representation. Market context modifies the surface through \(\mu_t=\Phi_c B\psi(m_t)\), through context-varying edge weights \(w_{ij}(m_t)\), or through a dynamic latent coefficient field on the graph. Plateaus are naturally encouraged because Laplacian quadratic penalties suppress local differences. True discontinuities, however, are blurred by construction. Interpolation to unseen grid points is poor unless you separately map new candidates into the graph or place the prior on a coordinate basis rather than directly on node values. Bayesian uncertainty is strong: Laplacian regularisation is a Gaussian Markov random field style prior with sparse precision, so posterior computation can exploit sparse linear algebra. Sequential updating is practical for Gaussian blocks and still manageable under approximate non-Gaussian filtering because sparse precision matrices scale well. For the BOLR grid, a 4-neighbour graph has \(2780\) undirected edges, so memory is trivial. A direct node-level model carries about \(N=1428\) latent scores per day unless compressed by a contextual basis, which is acceptable overnight but less nice if you later add many assets or particles. Identifiability issues come from the Laplacian nullspace, usually solved by anchoring the mean or adding \(\varepsilon I\). Failure modes are oversmoothing across genuine stop-trigger boundaries and lack of interpolation. For BOLR this is a **very useful prior on residual structure**, but too blunt as a stand-alone final architecture. The prototype should place a Laplacian prior on residual node effects after subtracting a contextual tensor-spline mean and compare it against i.i.d. residuals. fileciteturn0file1 fileciteturn0file2 citeturn0academia2turn0academia1turn7search5

**Graph total variation and discontinuity-aware priors.** Replace the quadratic penalty by an edge-difference \(\ell_1\) or heavy-tailed prior:
\[
p(s_t)\propto \exp\!\left(-\lambda \|D s_t\|_1\right),
\]
or hierarchically \(d_{e,t}=s_{i,t}-s_{j,t}\sim \text{Laplace}(0,b_e)\) or a horseshoe-type prior on \(d_{e,t}\). This assumes that the surface is **piecewise smooth or piecewise constant** on the grid, with a relatively small number of active boundaries. That is exactly the right inductive bias if you believe small parameter changes often do nothing and then suddenly change the trade path when an entry or trailing-stop threshold is crossed. Market context typically modifies the *mean surface* or the local shrinkage scales, so the location and sharpness of boundaries can change with regime. Plateaus are represented extremely well; discontinuities are the whole point of the model. Interpolation is weak in the pure graph form because the representation lives on observed nodes. Bayesian uncertainty is harder: posteriors are non-Gaussian and often multimodal around edge fusions, though scale-mixture constructions and modern variational or MCMC schemes make them tractable in moderate size. Sequential updating is possible, but not through a neat Kalman recursion; one usually alternates proximal/ADMM or variational updates overnight. Parameter count is \(N\) node values plus edge-wise local scales if using a hierarchical model. Complexity is roughly linear in \(|E|\) per proximal sweep but multiplied by iteration count; on a graph this small that is still practical. Identifiability is less of a problem than in factor models, but translation invariance and choice of boundary regularisation strength matter. Failure modes are staircase artefacts, unstable edge selection when signal is weak, and poor off-grid scoring. For BOLR this is **one of the best mechanisms for preserving plateaus and trading-mechanic discontinuities**, but it should almost certainly sit on top of a smoother contextual baseline. The right prototype is a graph-TV residual on top of a contextual spline mean, with comparison against a graph-Laplacian residual and a pure graph-TV surface. fileciteturn0file1 fileciteturn0file2 citeturn2academia0turn9academia3turn8academia0turn9search5

**Smooth-surface-plus-residual models.** This is not a single prior so much as the architecture most strongly supported by your notes:
\[
s_{it}=f_{\text{smooth}}(c_i,m_t)+r_{it},
\]
with \(f_{\text{smooth}}\) given by a tensor spline, low-rank surface, or separable GP, and \(r_t\) given by a sparse graph-Laplacian, graph-TV, or local heavy-tailed residual. The assumption is that most variation is smooth and context-driven, but a minority of behaviour reflects local threshold effects or candidate-specific deviations. Market context primarily deforms the smooth term, while the residual either stays weakly dynamic or receives its own smaller contextual modulation. This architecture has the best ability to represent both plateaus and discontinuities: the smooth term captures broad connected high-quality regions, while the residual sharpens boundaries or protects isolated local structure. Interpolation survives because the smooth term extrapolates inside the grid; the residual then acts as a local correction on observed nodes. Bayesian uncertainty can be split into a Gaussian smooth block plus a non-Gaussian residual block, which is exactly the kind of decomposition that supports practical sequential inference: use ADF/Laplace/Gaussian filtering for the smooth coefficients and an overnight proximal or variational step for the residual. Parameter count is additive; for example, an 80-basis contextual spline mean plus a 1428-node residual with strong shrinkage is still manageable. Complexity is also favourable because the expensive global component stays low-dimensional, while the node residual is sparse and local. Identifiability requires centring the residual, shrinking it aggressively, and preventing it from re-learning the smooth surface. Failure occurs when the residual is too flexible and collapses the whole model back to candidate IDs. For BOLR, this is the **best overall structural family**. The prototype experiment should be the anchor of the programme: compare smooth-only, smooth+Laplacian residual, and smooth+TV residual under identical walk-forward evaluation and runtime budgets. fileciteturn0file2 fileciteturn0file3 citeturn13academia3turn0academia2turn2academia0

## Contextual and hybrid interaction models

**Varying-coefficient contextual models.** The cleanest contextual formulation is
\[
s_{it}=\phi_c(c_i)^\top\big(\alpha+B\psi(m_t)\big)+\Gamma^\top z_{it},
\]
or equivalently \(s_{it}=\phi_c(c_i)^\top w(m_t)+\Gamma^\top z_{it}\). The assumption is that the market does not create entirely new surface structure each day; it changes the coefficients of a stable structural basis. This matches your core observation that context must modify relative ranking through interactions, not through a cancelled shared addend. Plateaus and discontinuities depend entirely on the chosen candidate basis: with splines it is smooth; with graph basis atoms it can be rougher. Interpolation is excellent if \(\phi_c\) is continuous. Bayesian uncertainty is attractive because the whole construction is linear in parameters once bases are fixed. Sequential updating is among the best of all methods: this is the most filter-friendly representation in the report, especially with Gaussian priors, shrinkage, and block-diagonal process noise. Parameter count is \(p_c + p_cH + p_z\), where \(p_c\) is the candidate-basis dimension. With \(p_c=80\), \(H=12\), \(p_z=16\), count is about \(1056\). Identifiability concerns are mild compared with factor models: centre \(\psi(m_t)\), regularise \(B\), and fix likelihood temperature or score scale. Failure modes are basis misspecification and context overparameterisation. Suitability for BOLR is excellent because it directly implements the design principle already articulated in your notes. The most informative prototype is to use the same candidate basis as the spline baseline, then add varying coefficients only on a selected small market basis and test whether contextual deformation materially reduces regret relative to a context-free surface. fileciteturn0file0 fileciteturn0file2 citeturn18academia1turn18search3turn18academia0

**Candidate–market latent factor models.** Here
\[
s_{it}=a(c_i)+u(c_i)^\top v(m_t)+\Gamma^\top z_{it},
\]
with low-dimensional embeddings \(u(c)\in\mathbb R^R\) and \(v(m)\in\mathbb R^R\). This is the compatibility or latent-factor view: the market selects a direction in latent space, and candidates whose embeddings align with that direction receive higher scores. The assumption is that the candidate–market interaction is intrinsically low-dimensional even if expressed in many raw features. Market context changes the surface through the low-dimensional vector \(v(m_t)\). Plateaus are only moderately well represented unless nearby candidates are constrained to have similar embeddings. Discontinuities are poor without an added graph or residual term. Interpolation depends on how \(u(c)\) is built: basis-based embeddings interpolate; ID-based embeddings do not. Bayesian uncertainty is feasible but more difficult than in varying-coefficient models because rotations and rescalings of the latent factors are not identified. Sequential updating is possible and cheap if \(R\) is small, but posterior approximations can become unstable when multiple latent configurations explain the same ranking equally well. Parameter count is roughly \(R(p_c+H)\) plus baseline terms for basis-driven models, or \(NR+HR\) for pure ID embeddings, which I would avoid. The failure mode for BOLR is subtle: these models often learn “soft clusters of good candidates” but can ignore the explicit two-dimensional adjacency that your EDA shows is highly informative. They are therefore not a first-choice surface model, but they are a very useful **controlled ablation for whether adjacency matters beyond low-rank interaction**. The prototype should compare a rank-\(R\) latent compatibility model with and without graph smoothness on the candidate embeddings, at \(R\in\{4,8,12\}\). fileciteturn0file1 fileciteturn0file2 citeturn13academia2turn13academia4turn5academia5

**Gated experts.** A context-gated mixture writes
\[
s_{it}=\sum_{k=1}^{K}\pi_k(m_t)\,f_k(c_i,z_{it}),
\qquad \sum_k \pi_k(m_t)=1,\ \pi_k\ge 0.
\]
The assumption is that the market alternates between a small number of recurrent ranking regimes, each with its own candidate surface. This is appealing for trading because “opening-volatility day”, “trend day”, and “compressed-range day” genuinely may prefer different regions of the grid. Market context modifies the surface through the gate \(\pi_k(m_t)\), which switches or blends experts. Plateaus and discontinuities *across days* are well captured because the mixture can jump from one surface to another when regime probabilities move. Spatial discontinuities *within a day* depend on the expert form; by itself gating is not a discontinuity prior over the configuration grid. Interpolation and uncertainty depend on the experts and whether you maintain a Bayesian posterior over both experts and gate. Sequential updating is feasible if the experts are simple and \(K\) is small, but label-switching and expert collapse are real identifiability problems. Parameter count is roughly \(K\) times the expert size plus gate parameters; even with small \(K\), this multiplies complexity quickly. The failure mode in BOLR is data fragmentation: about 4.5k daily contexts is not much once split over multiple experts, especially if the target top-set is sparse on many days. My view is that gated experts are **best as a second-stage enhancement after a strong single-surface baseline exists**, not as the first nonlinear contextual model. The prototype should keep \(K\in\{2,3,4\}\), use shared low-rank spline experts, place strong entropy or shrinkage regularisation on the gate, and compare whether regime switching improves adaptation after obvious drift episodes without harming stable-period performance. fileciteturn0file3 citeturn4search1turn3search5

**Modular RFF and product kernels.** The recommended RFF form is not a single generic map on \([c_i;m_t]\), but a modular one:
\[
s_{it}=\alpha^\top z_c(c_i)+z_c(c_i)^\top B\,z_m(m_t)+\Gamma^\top z_{it},
\]
where \(z_c\) and \(z_m\) are separate random-feature maps approximating \(K_c\) and \(K_m\), and \(z_c\otimes z_m\) approximates a product kernel. The assumption is that the unknown smooth similarity structure is better captured by stationary kernels than by hand-built splines, but that candidate and market spaces should still be represented separately to preserve sample efficiency and interpretability. Market context modifies the surface through the bilinear cross-term rather than through a cancellation-prone additive intercept. Plateaus are representable if candidate kernels are smooth enough; discontinuities are usually weak unless combined with a local residual or many high-frequency features. Interpolation is strong for continuous inputs. Bayesian uncertainty is simple if weights are given Gaussian priors, because the model becomes Bayesian linear regression in fixed random features. Sequential updating is therefore excellent. Parameter count depends on whether \(B\) is full or low-rank: a full \(D_c\times D_m\) interaction is usually too large, but a rank-\(R\) factorisation reduces it to \(R(D_c+D_m)\). For example \(D_c=64\), \(D_m=64\), \(R=8\) implies about \(1024\) interaction parameters; a full interaction would be \(4096\). Runtime is linear in feature dimension and easy to implement efficiently in C. Identifiability issues are mild compared with latent factors, but bandwidth selection is crucial and joint isotropic RFF on concatenated features is usually a mistake in heterogeneous spaces. For BOLR, modular RFF is a **good benchmark for nonlinear flexibility after structured models are in place**; joint RFF over all raw features is likely too data-hungry relative to the daily sample size. The prototype should compare modular product-kernel RFF against joint RFF at matched parameter counts and against the spline baseline, focusing on whether the extra flexibility produces real top-of-list gains or just more variance. fileciteturn0file2 fileciteturn0file3 citeturn4search0turn3academia3turn17academia1

## Comparative synthesis for BOLR

The core comparison is not “which method is most expressive?” but “which bias is least wrong for this specific online engineering problem?” On your data, three facts matter more than everything else: the grid is fixed and regular, local dependence is strong, and the exact best point is unstable while high-quality regions are broader and more persistent. That combination strongly favours methods that borrow strength locally and condition aggressively on context, but it does **not** favour fully local candidate-ID models or fully global smoothers used in isolation. fileciteturn0file1 fileciteturn0file2

From that perspective, the frontier is fairly clear. Tensor-product splines and varying-coefficient models are the best **base representations** because they respect the regular log-grid, give excellent interpolation, and are the easiest to update sequentially. Low-rank tensor factorisations are useful when you need to compress the smooth surface, but they are too restrictive as the sole mechanism for local trading-mechanic boundaries. Kronecker covariance structure is valuable when you want principled uncertainty and fast Gaussian algebra on the grid, but pure separability is unlikely to be rich enough on its own. Graph-Laplacian priors are excellent for local sharing on observed nodes and very attractive computationally, but they systematically blur exactly the kind of threshold boundaries that trading rules can create. Graph total variation fixes that boundary issue, but should not be asked to learn the entire contextual surface from scratch. Gated experts are appealing conceptually for regimes, yet they are data-hungry and come with non-trivial identifiability risk. Modular RFF is a strong nonlinear benchmark and probably the best kernelised alternative, but only after the structured geometry has already been exploited. citeturn15search0turn7academia0turn0academia2turn2academia0turn4search1turn4search0

The most important engineering conclusion is therefore this: **the final model family should not be a single-method winner**. BOLR is exactly the kind of problem where a hybrid dominates. The smooth part should be continuous and context-conditioned; the residual part should be sparse, local, and discontinuity-aware. In Bayesian terms, the most defensible decomposition is a Gaussian smooth block plus a graph-structured non-Gaussian correction, because that maps naturally onto sequential inference with different update schedules and different process-noise scales. This also matches your own provisional direction in the notes, which already points toward a structured surface plus dynamic candidate-specific correction rather than a generic black-box ranker. fileciteturn0file2 fileciteturn0file3

## Recommended architecture and prototype experiments

My main recommendation is to make the **engineering base model** a contextual tensor surface with a discontinuity-aware residual:
\[
s_{it}
=
\phi_c(c_i)^\top\big(\alpha+B\psi(m_t)\big)
+
\Gamma^\top z_{it}
+
r_{it},
\]
where \(\phi_c(c_i)=\phi_e(u_i)\otimes \phi_r(v_i)\), \(B\) is either full with strong shrinkage or low-rank factorised, and \(r_t\) follows either a sparse graph-Laplacian prior or, preferably in the more ambitious version, a graph-TV / heavy-tailed edge-difference prior. The first term tracks broad market-conditioned movement of the good region; the second preserves candidate-history information; the third protects sharp boundaries, plateaus, and local mechanics. This is the architecture most aligned with your data-generating story and with the EDA evidence. fileciteturn0file1 fileciteturn0file2

I would implement the research programme in four prototypes rather than ten independent model builds. **Prototype A** should be the contextual tensor spline only, because it gives the cleanest read on whether market-conditioned deformation helps beyond a static surface. **Prototype B** should compress A with a low-rank coefficient factorisation to test whether low-rank structure is a feature or a harmful bottleneck. **Prototype C** should add a graph-Laplacian residual to A, mainly as the easiest hybrid. **Prototype D** should replace that residual with graph total variation or a heavy-tailed edge-difference prior, because that is the version most likely to respect plateaus and threshold boundaries. If D wins materially, you then have a strong case for keeping a discontinuity module in production. citeturn15search0turn2academia0turn0academia2

For the contextual and nonlinear comparisons, I would *not* begin with gated experts or fully generic joint RFF. Instead, run two controlled ablations. The first is a **candidate–market latent factor ablation** against Prototype A at matched parameter counts; this is the direct test of whether low-dimensional compatibility captures enough structure without explicit grid adjacency. The second is a **modular product-kernel RFF ablation** against Prototype A and D, again at matched parameter counts, to test whether a smoother kernelised representation buys anything beyond structured bases. If RFF wins, use it as a complementary alternative; if it only ties while being harder to tune, keep the spline/graph family. citeturn13academia2turn13academia4turn4search0turn17academia1

The experiments should be judged on the evaluation contract already specified in your Stage 1 notes: true prequential operation, identical feature information, identical candidate set, top-1 business metrics first, then top-\(K\) diagnostics, regret, uncertainty calibration, and latency. I would add one synthetic stress test that is especially revealing for this model class: create a controlled simulator on the \(34\times 42\) grid in which the good region drifts smoothly for a while, then undergoes a regime jump and finally acquires a hard discontinuity boundary. That one test will sharply separate oversmoothed models, under-regularised local models, and hybrids. On the historical data, I would explicitly stratify analysis into days with broad top regions versus days with narrow top regions, and days with positive configurations versus no-positive days, because your target behaviour is different in each case. fileciteturn0file0 fileciteturn0file1

The final prioritised recommendations are these. First, make **contextual tensor splines with Bayesian shrinkage** the default baseline. Second, treat **smooth-surface-plus-residual** as the most likely production family. Third, use **graph total variation or heavy-tailed edge priors** when the goal is preserving plateaus and rule-induced boundaries. Fourth, use **low-rank tensor structure** only as a compression mechanism for the smooth block, not as the sole structural assumption. Fifth, keep **Kronecker covariance** in the toolbox for Gaussian residuals and uncertainty, not as a stand-alone answer. Sixth, push **gated experts** and large **modular RFF** models to the second wave of experiments, after the simpler structured hybrid has either succeeded or clearly plateaued. That sequence gives you the best chance of getting a practically strong model early, while still leaving room for more expressive contextual machinery if the initial frontier is not good enough.

# What we can now freeze
## Accepted design principles

1. Configuration coordinates should be represented in log-space.
2. BOLR should model a suitability surface rather than unrelated IDs.
3. Tensor-product bases are the primary simple representation.
4. Shared market context should deform the surface through varying coefficients.
5. Rolling candidate performance should enter as a separate component.
6. Smooth-plus-local-residual is the leading long-term family.
7. Low rank is primarily a compression mechanism.
8. Kronecker structure is primarily a computational mechanism.
9. Graph-Laplacian and graph-TV residuals should be compared.
10. RFF and gated experts are second-wave alternatives.
