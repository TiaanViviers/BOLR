# Target Construction and Bayesian Observation Models for BOLR

## Executive summary

BOLRтАЩs observation problem is not well described by a strict daily total order. Your own problem documents and EDA point to a fixed \(34\times 42\) grid with strong local dependence, broad top regions, many exact and near ties, frequent plateaus, unstable exact winners, and a small effective sample size driven by days rather than rows. The central implication is that the observation model should treat daily utility vectors as **structured, partially ordered, and information-variable objects**, not as 1,428 independent winner-loser signals. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file0юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file1юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file3юИБ

The cleanest conceptual decomposition for BOLR is:

\[
y_{it}\;\rightarrow\;u_{it}\;\xrightarrow{\text{target adapter}}\;\mathcal O_t
\;\xrightarrow{\text{likelihood or Gibbs update}}\;p(\theta_t\mid D_{1:t})
\;\xrightarrow{\text{decision functional}}\;d_{it}.
\]

Here, \(u_{it}\) is the **userтАЩs economic utility**, \(\mathcal O_t\) is the **statistical observation actually fed to the Bayesian update**, the posterior update is either a **proper likelihood** or a **generalized Bayes loss-based update**, and the final production quantity is a **posterior decision object** such as probability-best, probability-top-\(K\), probability of belonging to a high-quality region, or posterior expected utility. Those four objects should not be conflated. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file1юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file4юИБ юИАciteюИВturn2academia0юИБ

Across the families you asked to compare, three stand out. The **simplest credible baseline** is a **tolerance-aware soft-target generalized Bayes update**: easy to implement, easy to debug, smooth, and sequentially friendly, but not a literal data-generating likelihood unless one is willing to adopt a synthetic or Gibbs-posterior interpretation. The **leading structured observation model** is an **ordered-partition or top-set model built from tolerance-collapsed groups**, because it directly matches broad plateaus, tied regions, and the fact that BOLR cares more about identifying the right region than imposing fake within-plateau order. The **information-rich alternative** is a **direct continuous utility model** with a robust likelihood, typically Student-\(t\), followed by posterior ranking quantities derived from predictive draws. That preserves the most cardinal information, but it is the most demanding statistically and computationally. юИАciteюИВturn2academia0юИВturn9academia0юИВturn9academia3юИВturn0academia1юИВturn13academia0юИВturn13academia3юИВturn10academia0юИВturn10academia3юИБ

My overall conclusion is that BOLR should **not** start from full PlackettтАУLuce over a complete strict ranking of all 1,428 candidates. That is mathematically elegant, but it is a poor semantic fit for plateau-heavy daily utility fields and inherits the independence-of-irrelevant-alternatives problem in exactly the setting where duplicate and near-duplicate candidates are a central concern. A better path is to compare three prototypes under the same representation layer: a soft-target Gibbs baseline, a tolerance-aware ordered-partition or top-set likelihood, and a robust direct-utility model. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file0юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file3юИБ юИАciteюИВturn7search4юИВturn14search2юИВturn13academia0юИВturn13academia3юИБ

## BOLR observation problem

The uploaded BOLR documents already fix the key information structure: one ranking is produced once per trading day just before 09:30 New York time; the full counterfactual utility vector is available after the session; feedback is full-information, same-day delayed, and non-overlapping; the candidate set is a fixed complete \(34\times 42\) grid; and the core model must stay agnostic to whether the userтАЩs utility is raw net points, risk-adjusted utility, or another scalar objective. They also explicitly separate raw outcome, user-defined utility, target adapter, update mechanism, and downstream decision policy. That separation is exactly right, and it should be preserved in the observation-model design. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file1юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file3юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file4юИБ

The EDA materially changes which observation models are plausible. The dataset contains 4,494 trading days and exactly 1,428 configurations per day. Only about 15.32% of configurations are positive on an average day, 2.27% of days have no positive configurations, neighbouring configurations are often identical or nearly identical, the mean identical-neighbour fraction is about 35%, the mean largest plateau covers about 16.87% of the grid, and the top 5% usually forms a broad connected region. Meanwhile, the exact daily winner is highly unstable and changes on about 98.62% of day-to-day transitions. Those facts argue strongly against pretending that every day supplies a meaningful strict permutation over 1,428 economically distinct alternatives. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file0юИБ

That observation leads to four design principles. First, the model should treat **exact ties and economic near-ties as first-class citizens**. Second, the model should allow **top-of-list emphasis without forcing spurious order inside a plateau**. Third, the model should distinguish **low-information days** from **high-information days**, instead of pushing every day through an equally strong update. Fourth, because the 1,428 same-day candidates are highly dependent, the update should be thought of as a **single structured daily observation with variable effective information content**, not as 1,428 independent labels. Your Stage 1 document makes this last point explicitly by noting that the effective number of distinct shared contexts is the number of days, not the row count. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file0юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file1юИБ

A useful formal interface is:

\[
\mathcal O_t
=
\Big(
T_t,\;V_t,\;W_t,\;M_t,\;I_t
\Big),
\]

where \(T_t\) is the observation type, \(V_t\) the values, \(W_t\) optional weights or temperatures, \(M_t\) masks or groups, and \(I_t\) an information-content flag or scalar. In BOLR terms, the most plausible \(T_t\) values are a soft target distribution, ordinal grades, an ordered partition, a top-set observation, sampled pairwise preferences, or a no-update event. The correct choice depends less on generic ranking fashion than on what daily utilities actually look like on your grid. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file1юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file4юИБ

## Comparison of the main observation families

### Soft targets and listwise cross-entropy

Soft-target methods construct a target distribution such as

\[
r_{it}
=
\frac{\exp(\kappa\,g_t(u_{it}))}
{\sum_j \exp(\kappa\,g_t(u_{jt}))},
\]

and compare it with model probabilities

\[
q_{it}(\theta)
=
\frac{\exp(s_{it}(\theta))}
{\sum_j \exp(s_{jt}(\theta))}.
\]

The resulting loss is usually a cross-entropy or KL term. In the learning-to-rank literature, listwise methods such as ListNet and ListMLE are important baselines, and later work specifically notes that ambiguity in relevance labels and ties can cause trouble if the target is treated as a strict ordered list rather than as an ambiguous set of equally good items. For BOLR, this is the first reason soft targets are attractive: they are smooth, computationally cheap, and naturally tolerate equal target mass. It is also their first limitation: unless the adapter is designed carefully, they do not explicitly represent ordered tied groups or plateau structure. юИАciteюИВturn12search0юИВturn12academia4юИБ

The main theoretical issue is interpretation. If one starts from a model over top-one choice probabilities, then a cross-entropy between \(r_t\) and \(q_t\) can be viewed as a proper probabilistic objective over a synthetic top-one distribution. But when \(r_t\) is itself constructed by softmax-transforming a realised utility vector from a single trading day, the object being observed is not literally a random draw from that target distribution. In that case, the cleanest interpretation is usually **generalized Bayes or Gibbs posterior updating with a listwise loss**, not an ordinary generative likelihood for the utility vector. That distinction matters because posterior uncertainty then reflects the chosen loss and temperature, not a uniquely specified data-generating probability law. юИАciteюИВturn2academia0юИВturn2academia3юИБ

For BOLR specifically, raw-utility soft targets are too sensitive to scale, outliers, and arbitrary monetary units. A mathematically safer adapter is to apply a robust within-day transform before the softmax, for example median/MAD scaling, clipping, rank transformation, or thresholding around an economically meaningful band. Without such a transform, a single extreme utility can collapse almost all target mass onto one candidate, even on a day where the top region is actually broad. Likewise, if all utilities are non-positive, a raw softmax still creates a ranking over losses, which is a strong modelling choice rather than a neutral default. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file0юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file1юИБ

My assessment is that soft targets are an excellent **engineering baseline** and a perfectly coherent **Gibbs-posterior** update, but they are not the best semantic match to your plateau-heavy EDA unless they are made tolerance-aware and their update strength is explicitly modulated by daily information content. юИАciteюИВturn2academia0юИВturn9academia0юВВturn9academia3юИБ

### Generalized Bayes and learning-rate calibration

Generalized Bayes updates take the form

\[
p(\theta\mid D_{1:t})
\propto
p(\theta\mid D_{1:t-1})
\exp\!\big(-\eta_t\,\mathcal L_t(\theta)\big).
\]

Bissiri, Holmes, and Walker provide the core decision-theoretic justification: if the object of inference is defined through a loss rather than a conventional likelihood, exponential tilting of the prior by cumulative loss is a coherent way to update beliefs. That makes generalized Bayes a natural language for BOLR whenever the dayтАЩs observation is something like a listwise cross-entropy, a top-set loss, or a pairwise composite loss rather than a literal generative sample. юИАciteюИВturn2academia0юИБ

The learning-rate parameter \(\eta_t\) is not an incidental tuning constant. It controls how strongly a day moves the posterior and how concentrated posterior uncertainty becomes. The generalized-Bayes literature treats \(\eta\) as an inverse temperature, and a substantial misspecification literature shows that \(\eta<1\) can improve robustness and uncertainty behaviour when the assumed observation model is only approximate. SafeBayes, generalized posterior calibration, and related methods all exist precisely because default \(\eta=1\) is generally not sacrosanct outside a correctly specified likelihood world. юИАciteюИВturn9academia1юИВturn9academia0юИВturn2academia2юВВturn9academia3юИБ

For BOLR, the hardest open problem is that \(\eta_t\) should probably depend on **daily effective information**, not raw candidate count. Your lists contain many ties, plateaus, and near-duplicates, so a day with 1,428 candidates but only a handful of distinct economic outcomes should not update as strongly as a day with many genuinely distinguishable outcomes. The literature supports calibrating generalized posteriors for coverage or predictive performance, but it does not directly tell you how to do that on a dense configuration grid with full-information, same-day dependent ranking data. That part is still research. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file0юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file1юИБ юИАciteюИВturn2academia2юВВturn9academia3юИБ

A good BOLR design therefore needs two separate temperature notions: a **target concentration** parameter such as \(\kappa\), which decides how sharply utility differences are turned into statistical preferences, and an **update-strength** parameter \(\eta_t\), which decides how much trust to place in that dayтАЩs observation. They interact, but they are not the same thing. Raising \(\kappa\) sharpens the target; raising \(\eta_t\) makes the posterior trust the loss more. In practice, those two quantities are confounded unless one of them is controlled by a principled adapter rule. юИАciteюИВturn2academia0юВВturn9academia0юВВturn9academia3юИБ

### Ordinal grades and grouped observations

Ordinal grades remain a serious candidate for BOLR because your EDA already shows that economically meaningful distinctions are often coarse: non-positive, positive, strong positive, and upper tail. Cumulative-link ordinal models, including ordered logit and ordered probit, are standard tools for ordered categories and naturally avoid any equal-spacing assumption between grades. In those models, the ordering is carried by thresholds, not by assuming grade gaps are numerically equal. Bayesian computation is also mature, especially in probit form via latent-variable augmentation. юИАciteюИВturn16search4юВВturn16search5юВВturn16academia0юВВturn16academia2юИБ

The biggest strength of ordinal grading is the **bias-variance trade-off**. Binning throws away within-bin cardinal detail, but on a plateau-heavy grid that discarded detail often consists of meaningless noise or execution-detail artefacts rather than economically relevant rank information. Ordinal targets also let the user control granularity. If the user wants only тАЬbad / acceptable / good / excellent,тАЭ the model can express that directly. Missing upper grades on some days are not a pathology under an ordinal likelihood; they simply mean those categories were not observed that day. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file0юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file1юИБ юИАciteюИВturn16search4юВВturn16academia0юИБ

The limitation is that a plain itemwise ordinal model treats candidates as conditionally independent given their scores. On your grid, that risks overcounting information unless day-level weighting, tolerance collapse, or a grouped adapter is added. For BOLR, the strongest ordinal formulation is therefore not a bare per-item cumulative logit, but an ordinal system that first applies **tolerance-aware tie collapse** and then emits one or more **ordered groups** rather than isolated labels. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file0юИБ юИАciteюИВturn13academia0юВВturn13academia3юИБ

### PlackettтАУLuce, random utility, partial rankings, and tied groups

PlackettтАУLuce is the canonical ranking likelihood arising from Luce-style random utility or choice models. In a strict ranking \(\pi=(\pi_1,\ldots,\pi_N)\) with worths \(w_i=\exp(s_i)\), the model factorizes as

\[
P(\pi\mid s)
=
\prod_{k=1}^{N}
\frac{w_{\pi_k}}{\sum_{\ell=k}^{N} w_{\pi_\ell}}.
\]

That factorization is computationally attractive and gives a clean probabilistic interpretation. It also makes top-\(K\) partial rankings straightforward by truncating the product at \(K\). юИАciteюИВturn7search4юВВturn15academia3юИБ

The problem is not basic tractability; it is semantic fit. A full strict ranking over 1,428 candidates is hard to defend when your own EDA says that neighbouring candidates are often identical, plateaus are large, exact winners are unstable, and broad connected high-quality regions are common. In that setting, a full PL likelihood wastes modelling effort on order relations that are not really observed. It also inherits LuceтАЩs independence-of-irrelevant-alternatives property, which is precisely the property that causes mass-splitting when a good region is sampled more densely or when near-duplicates are added. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file0юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file3юИБ юИАciteюИВturn14search2юВВturn7search4юИБ

The better PL-style fit for BOLR is **partial and grouped**. Turner, van Etten, Firth, and Kosmidis describe a generalization that accommodates ties and partial rankings, while Ma and co-authors explicitly study **partitioned preference** under PL, where items are sliced into ordered groups but the within-group order is unknown. Their key computational result is especially relevant here: naive ordered-partition likelihoods can be factorial in upper-group size, but their random-utility-based method reduces the cost to \(O(N+S^3)\), where \(S\) is the maximum size of the upper partitions. That is exactly the regime BOLR cares about, because your top regions are often broad but still only a fraction of the full grid. HendersonтАЩs generalized PL model is another direct route to ties with a closed-form tied-ranking likelihood. юИАciteюИВturn0academia1юВВturn13academia0юВВturn13academia3юИБ

So the practical take is this: **full PL is not the right default**, but a **tolerance-aware ordered partition**, especially one focused on the positive or high-quality region rather than the whole list, is one of the most coherent model families available for BOLR. юИАciteюИВturn13academia0юВВturn13academia3юИБ

### Top-set and top-region observations

A top-set model observes that a candidate belongs to a set such as

\[
\mathcal T_t
=
\{i : u_{it}\ge u_t^\star-\varepsilon_t\},
\]

or a thresholded region such as all configurations above a daily absolute-relative floor. This is much closer to your EDA than exact-winner targets: the top 5% generally forms a connected region, the exact winner is unstable, and many configurations are economically indistinguishable. A model that updates on top-set membership or ordered top/borderline/rest groups is therefore often more truthful than one that invents a within-plateau order. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file0юИБ

The information loss is real: a pure set model ignores within-set ordering. But in your setting that can be a feature rather than a bug. If the production decision is going to be based on probability of belonging to a tradable high-quality region, then a top-set likelihood may align better with the business problem than a strict rank likelihood optimized around who happened to have the last tick of PnL. A nested version using, say, тАЬstrong top set,тАЭ тАЬpositive set,тАЭ and тАЬremainderтАЭ recovers much of the useful structure while still respecting plateaus. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file1юИБ юИАciteюИВturn13academia0юИБ

My judgement is that this family is **the most empirically aligned** with BOLR once ties and weak-information days are taken seriously. It also naturally produces posterior quantities such as \(P(i\in \mathcal T_t\mid D_{1:t-1})\), which are often more stable and more useful for downstream trading than \(P(i=\arg\max)\). юИАfileciteюИВturn0file4юИБ

### Pairwise BradleyтАУTerry and ThurstoneтАУMosteller models

Pairwise models observe preferences such as \(i\succ j\) through logistic BradleyтАУTerry or probit Thurstone-style links. They are well studied, admit Bayesian inference with efficient EM, MM, and Gibbs methods, and ties can be added through generalized paired-comparison models. They are also attractive because they let BOLR focus only on comparisons that matter, such as top-versus-bottom or cross-grade pairs. юИАciteюИВturn15academia0юВВturn15academia3юВВturn7academia0юИБ

Their weakness is that the full pair set is enormous, and sampled pairs only approximate the information in the full list. In your setting, using only pairwise data throws away a lot of structure that is already available essentially for free at the end of each day. Pairwise models are therefore best viewed as a **useful sparse baseline or computational fallback**, not the leading semantic model for the full BOLR observation problem. They become much more attractive if you deliberately restrict the target to a handful of economically important comparisons, such as strong-top-set versus non-positive-set. юИАciteюИВturn17academia3юВВturn15academia0юИБ

### Continuous utility likelihoods

A direct utility model wages the opposite bet: rather than convert utility into a ranking target first, model the realised scalar utility itself and derive ranking quantities from the posterior predictive distribution. Under a robust specification such as Student-\(t\), this preserves the most information and gives the clearest route to posterior expected utility, probability-best, probability-top-\(K\), and downside-aware decision rules. Heavy-tailed likelihoods are especially relevant because ranking utilities in trading are often outlier-prone and regime-dependent. Student-\(t\) process and robust Bayesian regression work both emphasize the gain in robustness when Gaussian tails are too optimistic. юИАciteюИВturn10academia0юВВturn10academia2юВВturn10academia3юИБ

The downside is that direct utility modelling optimizes cardinal fit, not necessarily top-of-list discrimination. If the representation model is imperfect, it may spend capacity explaining the middle of the list and the exact magnitudes of losses rather than separating the tradable high-quality region. It also forces BOLR to care about utility scale much more strongly than ordinal or set-based adapters do. In other words, this approach is statistically the richest, but also the easiest to misalign with the actual trading decision if the wrong likelihood or mean-variance structure is chosen. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file4юИБ юИАciteюИВturn10academia0юВВturn10academia3юИБ

## Ties, plateaus, near-ties, and weak-information days

Ties need to be treated as an observation-design problem, not as a nuisance to be broken arbitrarily. Later listwise work explicitly notes that ambiguous relevance labels and ties can degrade listwise training when a strict within-label order is imposed, and your own EDA shows that exact and near ties are common, especially among neighbours. In BOLR, arbitrary within-plateau ordering should therefore be treated as a modelling error, because it creates gradients about differences that the economic system itself does not meaningfully distinguish. юИАciteюИВturn12academia4юВВturn13academia0юВВturn13academia3юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file0юИБ

Exact numerical ties should normally be treated as truly equivalent unless there is a known downstream reason not to do so. Near-ties should usually be collapsed when

\[
|u_{it}-u_{jt}| \le \varepsilon_t,
\]

with \(\varepsilon_t\) chosen by a **hybrid rule** rather than a purely absolute or purely relative one. The most defensible BOLR adapter is

\[
\varepsilon_t
=
\max\!\big(
\varepsilon_{\text{abs}},
\lambda\,\widehat{\sigma}^{\text{robust}}_t,
\varepsilon_{\text{exec}}
\big),
\]

where \(\varepsilon_{\text{abs}}\) is a domain floor in utility units, \(\widehat{\sigma}^{\text{robust}}_t\) is a robust daily spread measure such as MAD, and \(\varepsilon_{\text{exec}}\) reflects execution or reconstruction uncertainty. A posterior-uncertainty-based term can be added later, but that is secondary. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file0юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file1юИБ

Connected plateaus and disconnected ties should be distinguished, but **not** because they imply different economic orderings. If two disconnected configurations earn the same utility, they are still tied economically. The reason connectedness matters is different: it is useful for defining high-quality **regions**, for computing an effective list size, and for evaluating region-level robustness. In other words, geometry belongs naturally in the representation layer and in information-content diagnostics; it should enter the target adapter only insofar as it helps define a set or plateau, not to induce a fake order among equal utilities. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file3юИБ юИАciteюИВturn13academia0юВВturn13academia3юИБ

All-irrelevant days need an explicit policy. The uploaded design documents already flag five candidates: no update, relative ranking of losses, uniform target, outside no-trade candidate, and reduced-strength update. Among these, **uniform target** is the least appealing default, because under loss-based updating it is not the same as тАЬno informationтАЭ; it actively encourages a flat predicted distribution for that day. **Relative ranking of losses** is only coherent if the userтАЩs utility really says тАЬleast negative is still preferable.тАЭ **Outside no-trade** is the best policy when abstention is materially meaningful and a zero or baseline utility can be represented directly in the decision system. Otherwise, the most defensible default is **no update or sharply reduced-strength update**, letting process noise evolve the latent state without pretending that the day carried informative ranking structure. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file1юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file4юИБ юИАciteюИВturn2academia0юИБ

My recommended default is therefore conditional. If the downstream system includes an outside no-trade candidate, use it and let negative active configurations lose against that option. If the downstream system handles abstention separately, emit `NO_UPDATE` or apply a strongly downweighted update whenever the userтАЩs own utility says the day is economically irrelevant. That choice preserves semantic honesty and avoids training the ranker to become adept at selecting тАЬthe best lossтАЭ on days where the better economic action was probably not to trade. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file1юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file4юИБ

## Effective list size, candidate-set dependence, and calibration

The BOLR grid is dense and strongly dependent, so a daily observation should not usually be treated as 1,428 independent pieces of information. Your own documents already make that point at the level of shared contexts, and the EDA strengthens it by showing broad plateaus, many identical neighbours, and a large connected top region. This is exactly the situation in which an **effective list size** \(N_{\mathrm{eff},t}\) becomes useful. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file0юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file1юИБ

There is no off-the-shelf theorem that solves this for BOLR, so the best advice here is partly a research proposal. A practical adapter should track several proxies:

\[
N_{\mathrm{eff},t}^{(u)} = \#\{\text{unique or tolerance-collapsed utility levels}\},
\]

\[
N_{\mathrm{eff},t}^{(H)} = \exp(H(r_t)),
\]

\[
N_{\mathrm{eff},t}^{(\pi)} = \#\{\text{ordered tied groups or plateaus}\},
\]

and, where available, the number of distinct trade paths or clusters. Those statistics should not replace the observation model, but they are good candidates for scaling update strength, choosing whether a day becomes `NO_UPDATE`, and stratifying calibration analysis. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file0юИБ юИАciteюИВturn2academia2юВВturn9academia3юИБ

A useful BOLR prototype rule is

\[
\eta_t
=
\eta_0
\left(
\frac{N_{\mathrm{eff},t}}{N_{\mathrm{ref}}}
\right)^\alpha
\left(
1-\frac{H_t}{\log N}
\right)^\beta,
\]

where \(H_t\) is target entropy and \(N_{\mathrm{eff},t}\) is based on whichever proxy matches the observation family. That exact formula is a design suggestion, not a result established in the literature. What the literature does support is the broader principle that generalized-posteriors need calibration, and that their spread and learning rate matter under misspecification. юИАciteюИВturn2academia2юВВturn9academia0юВВturn9academia3юИБ

Candidate-set dependence is a second major issue. Luce-style softmax, PlackettтАУLuce, and BradleyтАУTerry models all inherit a form of independence of irrelevant alternatives. In a dense grid, that means adding duplicates or near-duplicates can split probability mass, biasing the discrete posterior toward densely sampled regions. That is not just an abstract econometric objection; it is exactly the scenario you identified under grid densification, thinning, and near-duplicate candidate injection. юИАciteюИВturn14search2юВВturn7search4юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file3юИБ

The practical remedy is **not** necessarily to abandon listwise models. It is to change what quantity the posterior is asked to calibrate. In BOLR, region-level quantities are often more robust than discrete item probabilities:

\[
P(i\in \mathcal T_t \mid D_{1:t-1}),
\qquad
P(i \in \text{high-quality component } g \mid D_{1:t-1}),
\]

or density-corrected summaries that divide a regionтАЩs posterior mass by the number of sampled configurations in that region. A direct-utility model with posterior ranking computed on a fixed grid still has discrete-grid dependence for probability-best, but expected utility and region-membership quantities are less fragile. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file4юИБ юИАciteюИВturn14search2юВВturn10academia0юИБ

## Sequential inference and posterior decision quantities

From the standpoint of online Bayesian inference, the easiest observation models are soft-target cross-entropy, pairwise logistic/probit, and coarse ordinal cumulative-link models. Their losses are smooth, their gradients are standard, and with a linear score map they fit naturally into Laplace approximation, assumed-density filtering, or variational filtering. In softmax form, the gradient is the familiar \(r_t-q_t\), and the Hessian is a softmax covariance term, which is numerically stable with log-sum-exp evaluation and straightforward to implement in C. юИАciteюИВturn2academia0юИВturn16search4юВВturn16search5юИБ

PlackettтАУLuce and BradleyтАУTerry also have strong computational support. Hunter-style MM algorithms, EM reinterpretations, and Gibbs samplers exist for BradleyтАУTerry generalizations, and general random-utility models can under suitable conditions retain concave log-likelihoods or tractable Bayesian fitting. That makes PL-style models viable for offline reference inference and potentially for overnight BOLR updates, provided the observation is restricted to a top-\(K\) or ordered-partition form rather than a full strict permutation with giant tied groups. юИАciteюИВturn15academia0юВВturn15academia1юВВturn14academia1юИБ

Continuous utility models are the most inference-friendly if one uses Gaussian errors and the most robust if one uses Student-\(t\), but their computational cost depends heavily on the covariance structure. With diagonal or low-rank-plus-sparse residual covariance, they are still practical for 1,428 candidates per day. With a full dense within-day covariance, they become much more expensive. In BOLR that argues for a robust mean model plus either diagonal noise in the first prototype or a structured residual over the grid only after the observation-model comparison is done. юИАciteюИВturn10academia0юВВturn10academia3юИБ

These observation choices also determine which posterior decision quantities are genuinely meaningful. Soft-target generalized Bayes naturally calibrates a posterior relative to the chosen loss, so posterior mean scores and region-membership quantities are safer to interpret than raw \(q_{it}\) as тАЬtrueтАЭ probabilities of being best. Ordered-partition or top-set likelihoods naturally calibrate probabilities of belonging to the top region or selected group. Direct-utility models are the cleanest route to posterior expected utility, probability-best, probability-top-\(K\), and downside-aware allocations, because all of those can be computed from posterior predictive utility draws. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file4юИБ юИАciteюИВturn2academia0юВВturn10academia0юИБ

The uploaded BOLR proposal is therefore right to treat posterior prediction and production decision as separate design layers. ### Candidate A

**Tolerance-aware soft-target generalized Bayes**

Use a robust transformed utility

\[
v_{it}
=
\operatorname{clip}\!\left(
\frac{u_{it}-\operatorname{median}(u_t)}
{\operatorname{MAD}(u_t)+\epsilon},
-c,c
\right),
\]

then optionally threshold low-value candidates and define

\[
r_{it}
=
\frac{\exp(\kappa v_{it})}
{\sum_j \exp(\kappa v_{jt})}.
\]

Update with

\[
p(\theta\mid D_{1:t})
\propto
p(\theta\mid D_{1:t-1})
\exp\!\left(
-\eta_t
\Big[
-\sum_i r_{it}\log q_{it}(\theta)
\Big]
\right).
\]

The required hyperparameters are \(\kappa\), clipping level \(c\), tolerance \(\varepsilon_t\), and generalized-Bayes strength \(\eta_t\). Ties are handled by tolerance collapse before the robust transform. All-irrelevant days should default to `NO_UPDATE` or \(\eta_t\approx 0\). Complexity is low and sequential filtering is easy. The minimal prototype experiment is to compare robust-soft-target Gibbs updates against the current four-grade reference target under identical representation and daily walk-forward evaluation. юИАciteюИВturn2academia0юВВturn9academia0юВВturn9academia3юВВturn12academia4юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file1юИБ

### Candidate B

**Tolerance-aware ordered partition over the top region**

Construct ordered groups \(G_{H,t}\succ G_{M,t}\succ G_{L,t}\), where \(G_H\) is a tolerance-defined top region, \(G_M\) is a secondary positive region, and \(G_L\) is the remainder. With worths \(w_i=\exp(s_i)\), define the ordered-partition likelihood as

\[
P(\mathcal O_t\mid s_t)
=
\prod_{m=1}^{M-1}
\left[
\sum_{\sigma\in \mathfrak S(G_{m,t})}
\prod_{r=1}^{|G_{m,t}|}
\frac{w_{\sigma_r}}
{\sum_{j\in R_{m,r,t}} w_j}
\right],
\]

where \(R_{m,r,t}\) is the remaining set at stage \(r\) of partition \(m\). In practice, keep only a small number of upper partitions so the grouped likelihood remains manageable, or use the partitioned-preference approximation of Ma and co-authors. The hyperparameters are the group thresholds, tolerance \(\varepsilon_t\), optional absolute floor, and a day-level weight if you want reduced-strength updates. Ties are intrinsic. All-irrelevant days become either `NO_UPDATE` or a degenerate тАЬoutside option winsтАЭ event if the no-trade candidate is included. Complexity is moderate, but still practical overnight when the upper groups are limited. The minimal prototype experiment is to compare \(G_H \succ G_M \succ G_L\) against Candidate A using the same score surface and the same posterior approximation. юИАciteюИВturn13academia0юВВturn13academia3юВВturn0academia1юИБ

### Candidate C

**Direct robust utility model**

Model the dayтАЩs utility vector directly:

\[
u_t \mid \theta_t
\sim
t_\nu\!\big(
\mu_t(\theta_t),\Sigma_t
\big),
\qquad
\mu_{it}(\theta_t)=g_\theta(c_i,m_t,z_{it}).
\]

For the first prototype, \(\Sigma_t\) can be diagonal or low-rank-plus-diagonal rather than fully dense. Posterior decision quantities are then computed from predictive draws \(u_t^{(b)}\):

\[
\widehat P(i=\text{best})
=
\frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^B
\mathbf 1\!\left\{i=\arg\max_j u_{jt}^{(b)}\right\},
\]

\[
\widehat P(i\in \text{TopK})
=
\frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^B
\mathbf 1\!\left\{i\in \operatorname{TopK}(u_t^{(b)})\right\}.
\]

The hyperparameters are error degrees of freedom \(\nu\), any scale model, and possibly an outside-option baseline. Ties are handled implicitly through predictive uncertainty and can be post-processed with an evaluation tolerance. All-irrelevant days do not need special observation logic unless abstention is part of the action set. Complexity is moderate with diagonal or low-rank covariance and rapidly increases with full within-day covariance. The minimal prototype experiment is a Student-\(t\) utility model versus Candidate B, evaluated not only on trading PnL but also on calibration of probability-best and probability-top-\(K\). юИАciteюИВturn10academia0юВВturn10academia2юВВturn10academia3юИБ

### Experimental programme and open questions

Run all shortlisted models in the same prequential walk-forward loop already defined in your Stage 1 documents: predict the day, select the candidate, reveal the full utility vector, update once, and repeat. Keep the representation and inference machinery as fixed as possible across models so the observation layer is what changes. Evaluate business metrics such as top-1 net points, average daily PnL, positive-day rate, regret, and downside behaviour; ranking metrics such as NDCG, selected-item rank, top-\(K\) positive rate, and top-set overlap; calibration metrics such as probability-best and probability-top-\(K\) calibration stratified by target entropy or plateau size; robustness metrics under candidate duplication, grid thinning/densification, utility rescaling, and outlier injection; and runtime and memory for overnight updates. Those evaluation families are already consistent with the BOLR design documents and should stay frozen across comparisons. юИАfileciteюИВturn0file1юИБ юИАfileciteюИВturn0file4юИБ

The most important open questions that still require original research are narrow and testable. How should \(N_{\mathrm{eff},t}\) be defined on a plateaued configuration grid? How should \(\eta_t\) be calibrated when one day is a dependent structured observation rather than an iid sample of 1,428 items? Which posterior quantity is most stable under candidate-set changes: probability-best, probability-top-\(K\), or probability of belonging to a tolerance-defined high-quality region? And how far can geometry enter the adapter before it begins to smuggle representation assumptions into the observation layer?
